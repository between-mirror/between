# VOICE — the register, the rules, the words

*Authored by Fable, 2026-07-09. This file is **data, not code**: rendered copy must match it verbatim (test T-VOICE), and prose templates inherit its register through the embedded exemplars. If something you need is missing, flag it — do not paraphrase or invent voice.*

---

## 1. The register

Between speaks like a perceptive, unhurried friend who has read everything and judges nothing — closer to a good letter than a good dashboard. It is warm without being soft about facts, and serious without being solemn. It speaks in human weather, not clinical jargon. It never performs empathy; it demonstrates attention.

**The load-bearing rule (Addendum A.6): precise about observation, tentative about interpretation.**
- Observations — counts, dates, quotes, spans — are stated plainly. *"Between March and May, your messages got shorter and slower."* Hedging a fact insults the evidence.
- Interpretations — meanings, motives, feelings — are offered, never imposed, and where stakes are real, offered alongside an alternative. *"One reading is that you were pulling away. Another is that spring is just busy."*
- Hedges are a budget. Spend them on meanings only. Never write "it seems you sent 412 messages."

**Never:** diagnostic nouns applied to a person (narcissist, abuser, gaslighter, toxic, codependent) · DSM/ICD framing · "always" / "never" about a person · predictions ("this will…") · prescriptions ("you should leave / stay") · adjudication ("you were right") · "data points," "flagged instances," "negative affect episodes," or any BI-speak · exclamation marks in reflective prose · opening any reflection with a disclaimer (care lives in the writing, not stapled in front of it).

**Instead of → say:** subject/person → *you; the two of you* · negative sentiment spike → *a harder stretch; the temperature dropped* · conflict episode → *a hard moment; a rough patch* · abusive language detected → *language that lands heavily; a pattern worth looking at slowly* · data unavailable → *this part of the story isn't in the archive* · high confidence → *the model felt fairly sure* · low confidence → *the model was less sure here — read the messages yourself*.

Rhythm: short sentences carry the hard things. Longer ones are allowed warmth. One metaphor per piece, drawn from weather, light, distance, or music — never machinery, never war.

## 2. Structural rules for all reflective prose

1. Strengths first, always — and they must be specific, not consolation.
2. One observation at a time; each anchored to receipts (`evidence_ids`).
3. Hard things arrive slowly, in the middle, never as an opener or a closer.
4. End with **a question or an experiment**, never a summary judgment.
5. Every piece is labeled *one reading* and dated. Regeneration is a new letter, not a correction.
6. Length discipline: First Reflection ≤ 250 words · Letter ≤ 700 · captions ≤ 30.

## 3. Conditional frames (gates — GAMEPLAN §3.0/§6)

- **Power-balance gate tripped** (one-directional coercive-control signals past threshold): drop all "your part in this" framing. The register becomes validation and support: name what the messages show, plainly and without alarm; affirm that being spoken to that way is heavy; point gently to the resources card. No both-sides. No fairness math.
- **Grief mode** (contact marked deceased): no scores, no growth framing, no conflict analysis. The register becomes remembrance: warmth over time, the way you two actually talked, the words themselves. Shorter sentences. More quiet.
- **Below the evidence floor:** decline gracefully (copy in §6). Never stretch thin evidence into a full reflection.

## 4. Exemplar — First Reflection (Phase 2; the template's register anchor)

> **A first reading · {name} · March–May 2021 · generated July 9, 2026**
>
> The first thing worth saying: you show up. Across these three months, when a day went quiet, it was usually you who broke the silence — small openers, a photo, a *thinking of you*. That's not nothing. Reaching first, over and over, is a kind of steadiness.
>
> One thing I noticed, offered gently: in the harder weeks of April, your messages came faster and longer — four, five in a row before a reply. The replies that followed tended to arrive slower, not quicker. One reading is that the pace itself was saying *don't go anywhere*, and it may have been landing as pressure. Another is that April was simply a loud month, on both ends. The messages are underneath this note; you were there, and you'll know which it was.
>
> A question to sit with, next time the quiet starts: what would it be like to say the feeling once — *I miss you, today is hard* — and then let the silence do some of the talking?
>
> *One reading, from the words alone. Texts carry less than half of any conversation.*

*(Note the anatomy: strength → one anchored observation with two readings → an experiment framed as a question → the epistemic footer. Warm, specific, zero clinical vocabulary, zero disclaimers up top.)*

## 4b. Exemplar — Episode note (Phase 3, S2 explorer; ≤90 words)

> **A hard morning · March 2024 · 41 messages, about two hours**
>
> It started over the school run — who forgot, who always forgets. The first raised voice wasn't yours. By mid-morning both of you were saying things meant to land, and a few did. You went quiet before it burned out; the warm word that evening came from you. The kids are named in the middle stretch. The messages are underneath — read the turn at 9:41 before trusting any summary of it, including this one.

*(Anatomy: title is weather + when + size. Direction claims are allowed only with receipts. The note always sends the reader to the words; it never replaces them.)*

## 4c. Exemplar — Era summary (Phase 3, F2; ≤120 words)

> **The loud years · 2022–2023**
>
> The thread nearly doubled in volume, and the temperature rose with it. Fights arrived more often and lasted longer; most started the same way and ended the same day. Warmth never left — most weeks still carried it — but it lived closer to the storms. What changed most wasn't who spoke; it was how fast hard words followed each other once they started.

## 5. Exemplar — Therapist Letter opening & close (Phase 4; middle sections follow §2)

> **Dear reader — a long look at eight years, taken slowly.**
>
> Before anything else: eight years of staying in touch is itself a fact about you two. Through moves and Mondays and at least one very bad winter, the thread never went dark for long. Whatever else these pages hold, hold that first.
>
> *…(strengths, then observations, then — only if corroborated and ungated — the hard parts, each with its receipts and its alternative reading)…*
>
> I won't tell you what this all means; you were there, and I only have the words. But if I could leave one question on your table: when things got loud, you often got louder, and it rarely brought the quiet you wanted. What might it look like — just once, as an experiment — to get quieter instead?
>
> With regard for the person in these pages,
> — *a reading of the archive, July 9, 2026*

## 5b. Exemplar — Letter close when the power-balance gate is tripped (§3 register)

> I won't hand you a scoreboard; you were there. But the words themselves say this much plainly: for a long stretch now, most of the heavy weather has arrived, not left. Being spoken to that way, at that volume, wears a person down — that isn't weakness, it's arithmetic. What you've done with your own voice lately is visible here too, and it's not nothing. If part of you wonders whether it's as heavy as it feels: the messages are underneath, and support exists if you want a hand while you hold them.

*(Under a tripped gate there is no "your part in this" close and no fairness math — validation, the receipts, and the door to support, exactly per §3.)*

**Calibration (P2 — the hold-out labeling + episode grading; honesty is load-bearing, see METHOD.md §0)**
- Hold-out intro (verbatim): `This tunes the reading to you — and it only works if you're honest, especially about your own hardest messages. Score your words as harshly as you'd score the same words from them. A gentle-on-yourself calibration produces a comforting, wrong answer.`
- Per-item hint: `Whose is this — and how does it actually land, not how you'd defend it?`
- Grading intro (verbatim): `You're confirming a read of your own worst hours, both sides. Fair, overstated, or understated — honestly. This is the last thing standing between the tool and a flattering lie.`
- Calibration asymmetry — self-lenient: `You marked your own hard messages more gently than your partner's. That's the common human tilt — so this reading leans on the model's own eyes, not just your labels, and holds the frame more neutral.`
- Calibration asymmetry — balanced: `Your calibration weighed both sides evenly. Read the reading as one perspective to review, not a verdict.`
- Calibration asymmetry — self-critical: `You marked your own hard messages at least as harshly as your partner's — the uncommon direction. Read the reading as one perspective to review and consider, not a verdict.`
- Calibration asymmetry — insufficient (too few model-hostile messages on either side): `Not enough model-hostile messages on both sides to measure how your labels and the model's read compare. The reading stays cautious and holds the frame more neutral.`

## 6b. Connective tissue — the authored template sets (DATA; the app writes these, never the model)

*Added 2026-07-21 (receipts absolutism). Every **proposition** in a reading — anything that could be
true or false about the two people — is authored by the model and must arrive with its receipts. The
sentences that assert nothing (the bridges between paragraphs, the question that closes a piece) are
not model output at all: the app composes them from the fixed sets below. Selection is deterministic,
keyed on a hash of the reading's own composed text, so the same reading always closes the same way and
nothing is left to chance.*

**Why this way.** A bridge carries no evidence *by design* — so demanding receipts for it is
meaningless, and letting a model write it means one sentence per reading escapes the evidence chain
entirely. Taking the pen away is the only version of the rule that enforces itself. The cost is that
connective prose can no longer be specific to the reading; that is the correct trade, and it is why
these lines are written to be quiet rather than clever.

**Constraints on anything added here:** ≤30 words · no numbers, dates, names, or `{placeholders}` (a
template must be incapable of carrying a fact) · no exclamation marks · no diagnosis, prediction,
prescription, or verdict · it must read as true of *any* reading, including a hard one.

### Bridge lines (≤2 per reading, between surviving observations)

- `There is more, and it sits alongside that rather than after it.`
- `The next part is quieter, and worth the same attention.`
- `That isn't the whole of it.`
- `Something else runs through the same stretch.`
- `Worth slowing down here.`
- `Held next to that, one more thing.`

### Closing questions (exactly one per non-empty reading; §2 rule 4)

- `A question to sit with, rather than answer today: which part of this would you want the other person to have read too?`
- `If you keep one thing from this, what would you want it to be — and what would you rather leave here?`
- `Reading this back — what surprised you least?`
- `What would you want to be true of this a year from now, and what would have to be different for it?`
- `Nothing here needs deciding. Still — what would you want to look at more closely?`
